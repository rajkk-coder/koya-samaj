Microplastic Occurrence in Food

Table Salt

As table salt is most often produced by distillation of seawater, it is difficult to avoid microplastics in the final products without additional purification steps since seawater contains microplastics [2,28]. Usually, hydrogen peroxide (H2O2) was used to digest organic matter in the solution after dissolving sea salts[14,15,29,30]. Typically, sodium iodide (NaI) was used for density separation up to 1.8 g cm*3 of solution density[30,31] When microplastics were counted visually under a dissection microscope with or without staining (e.g., Rose Bengal) [32], the resulting concentrations were usually higher than those measured under a microscope and Fourier-transform infrared spectroscopy (FT-IR), indicating a potential false-positive count. Large pore sizes (149 μm) resulted in significantly lower microplastic concentrations in both sea salts and lake salts[31]. Due to the experimental size cutoff at a lower range (0.2–11 μm), no significant differences in microplastic concentrations were found. Microplastics less than 10 μm would be very difficult to detect using stereo microscopes coupled with FT-IR spectroscopy[37]. Although rock salts and lake salts contained more fibers than sea salts, their microplastic levels were not significantly different from those of sea salts [14].
